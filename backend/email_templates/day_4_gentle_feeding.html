üìñ Rachel's Story: Gradually Separating Feeding from Sleep

The Situation:

Rachel's 5-month-old son, Ethan, would only fall asleep while nursing. Every nap, every bedtime, every night waking required nursing. Rachel was exhausted and touched-out.

But Rachel didn't want to use cry-it-out. She wanted a gentler approach, even if it took longer.

"I knew it would be slower," Rachel said. "But I needed to feel good about the process."

The Approach:

Week 1: Rachel started feeding with lights on, then doing a short bedtime routine before putting Ethan down drowsy. He fussed for 10-15 minutes while she patted and shushed him.

Week 2: She gradually reduced the patting and shushing. Ethan was falling asleep with less help, but still needed her presence.

Week 3: Rachel started sitting in a chair next to the crib instead of standing over him. Ethan protested at first but adapted within a few days.

Week 4: She moved the chair closer to the door. Then out the door. Then down the hall.

The Result:

By week 5, Ethan was falling asleep independently. Rachel could nurse him, put him in the crib awake, and walk out. He'd fall asleep on his own within 10-15 minutes with minimal fussing.

Night wakings reduced from 4-5 per night to 1-2 (for actual feedings). By week 8, he was sleeping 8-10 hour stretches.

"It took longer than CIO would have," Rachel said. "But I felt good about every step. And the results have lasted - no regression, no trauma, just gradual learning."
