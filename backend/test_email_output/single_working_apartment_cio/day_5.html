Sleep Training Solo: Your Approach

You don't have a partner to tap out to at 3 AM. That's real, and I'm not going to pretend it isn't harder.

What to know going in:
- You need to be extra committed - there's no backup
- Self-care between nights is non-negotiable
- The payoff is huge: better sleep for BOTH of you

Survival strategies:
- Set everything up before bedtime (water, snacks, phone charged)
- Have a "tap-out" plan for your hardest moment (call a friend, step outside for 30 seconds)
- Use a timer - knowing actual crying time helps
- Remind yourself: this is temporary

On your check-ins:
- You're it - keep them brief and consistent
- Don't let guilt make you linger
- Trust your method

After the first night:
- Debrief with yourself: what worked, what was hardest?
- Adjust as needed (timing, your position)
- Celebrate that you did it

You're proving that you can handle hard things alone. Which, honestly, you already knew. This is just the latest example.

CIO in an Apartment: You Can Do This

I know what you're worried about: the neighbors.

Here's what I need you to hear: most apartment walls muffle sound more than you think. Your baby's crying probably sounds louder to YOU (because parent guilt amplifies everything) than it does to the person next door.

Practical steps:
- White noise on BOTH sides of the shared wall helps
- A quick heads-up to neighbors buys goodwill: "We're sleep training this week - sorry in advance for any noise"
- Most neighbors are understanding (they were babies once too)

Perspective check:
- A few nights of crying beats 12+ months of exhaustion
- Your baby learning to sleep benefits everyone
- The crying WILL decrease - often dramatically by night 3

If anxiety is overwhelming:
- Set a timer - knowing actual crying time vs. perceived helps
- Step outside your apartment door to hear what neighbors actually hear
- Remember: you're not being cruel, you're teaching a skill

You're allowed to prioritize your family's sleep health. A few nights of noise is not a reason to stay trapped in exhaustion.

The truth is, sleep training isn't about leaving your baby to suffer. It's about giving them the gift of independent sleep - a skill that will serve them for life. You're not abandoning them. You're teaching them.

Since your baby currently needs nursing or a bottle to fall asleep, they're going to be confused when you put them down without it. That confusion = crying. But you're not taking away comfort - you're teaching them a new way to find it.

And yes, there might be tears tonight. Theirs and yours. That's normal. That doesn't mean you're failing.

What to Expect Tonight: 4-12 Month Olds

Here's the truth about what might happen:

Night 1:
- Expect 20-45 minutes of crying at bedtime (could be more)
- Multiple night wakings with crying
- This is the hardest night - for YOU

Night 2:
- Often slightly better OR slightly worse (extinction burst)

Night 3:
- Most babies show significant improvement here
- Bedtime crying often drops to under 15 minutes
- Fewer night wakings

By Night 5-7:

- Night wakings should be minimal or gone
- You should be seeing real progress

Your baby's age factors:

- 10-12 months: May stand in crib (lay them down once, then leave)

Important: Progress isn't linear. A bad Night 4 doesn't erase progress. Trust the process.
