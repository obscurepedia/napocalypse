How Sarah Used CIO to Break the Feed-to-Sleep Cycle in 7 Days

Real results from a real mom | 5-month-old baby | Cry-It-Out method

Sarah's Situation: Her 5-month-old daughter, Emma, would only fall asleep while nursing. Every single nap and bedtime required nursing. Emma was waking 4-6 times per night, needing to nurse back to sleep each time. Sarah was exhausted, touched-out, and desperate for change.

The Breaking Point

"I couldn't do it anymore," Sarah told me during our interview. "I was nursing Emma to sleep 8-10 times a day. Every nap, every bedtime, every night waking. I felt like a human pacifier."

Sarah's breaking point came at 3am on a Tuesday night. She'd been up every 90 minutes nursing Emma back to sleep. Her nipples were sore. Her back ached from the nursing position. And she realized she couldn't remember the last time she'd slept more than 2 hours straight.

"I was crying while Emma was nursing," Sarah said. "I knew something had to change. I couldn't keep living like this."

Why Sarah Chose Cry-It-Out

Sarah had tried gentler methods for weeks. She'd attempted to gradually reduce nursing time. She'd tried putting Emma down drowsy but awake. Nothing worked.

"Every time I tried to put her down without nursing her fully to sleep, she'd scream," Sarah explained. "I'd end up nursing her anyway. I was just prolonging the inevitable."

After researching sleep training methods, Sarah decided on Cry-It-Out (CIO) for three reasons:

  1. Speed: She needed results fast. She couldn't sustain another month of sleep deprivation.
  2. Clarity: CIO had clear rules. No gray areas. No "should I go in or not?" decisions at 2am.
  3. Research: She'd read the studies. CIO is safe, effective, and doesn't cause long-term harm.

The 7-Day Journey: Night by Night

Night 1: The Hardest Night

Bedtime: Sarah nursed Emma with the lights on, then did their bedtime routine. She put Emma in the crib awake and walked out.

What happened: Emma cried for 45 minutes. Sarah sat outside the door, timing it, crying herself.

Night wakings: Emma woke 5 times. Sarah didn't nurse her. Emma cried 20-30 minutes each time before falling back asleep.

Sarah's thoughts: "I almost gave up at minute 40. My husband had to physically stop me from going in. I felt like the worst mother in the world."

Night 2: Still Hard, But Progress

Bedtime: Emma cried for 35 minutes. Ten minutes less than night 1.

Night wakings: Still 5 times, but crying reduced to 15-25 minutes each time.

Sarah's thoughts: "I could see it was working. The crying was getting shorter. But it was still so hard to listen to."

Night 3: The Extinction Burst

Bedtime: Emma cried for 25 minutes. Progress!

Night wakings: This is where things got worse before they got better. Emma woke 7 times (more than before). She was testing the new system.

Sarah's thoughts: "I thought I'd broken her. She was waking MORE, not less. I was ready to quit. But I'd read about the extinction burst, so I stayed consistent."

Night 4: The Breakthrough

Bedtime: Emma cried for only 12 minutes. Sarah couldn't believe it.

Night wakings: Emma woke once at 2am, fussed for 5 minutes, then went back to sleep. Then she slept until 6:30am.

Total sleep: Emma slept for 6 hours straight - her longest stretch EVER.

Sarah's thoughts: "I woke up at 6am in a panic. I thought something was wrong. Then I realized she'd just slept through the night. I cried happy tears."

Night 5: Confirmation

Bedtime: 8 minutes of fussing (not even crying).

Night wakings: One brief wake at 3am. Back to sleep in 3 minutes.

Total sleep: 8 hours straight.

Sarah's thoughts: "This is real. It's actually working. I'm getting my life back."

Night 6: Almost There

Bedtime: 5 minutes of fussing.

Night wakings: None. Emma slept 10 hours straight.

Sarah's thoughts: "I kept checking the monitor to make sure she was breathing. I couldn't believe she was actually sleeping."

Night 7: Success

Bedtime: No crying. Emma went down awake, rolled to her side, and fell asleep within 5 minutes.

Night wakings: None. 11 hours straight.

Sarah's thoughts: "I have my evenings back. I'm sleeping through the night. Emma is happier during the day because she's well-rested. This changed our lives."

The Results: 2 Weeks Later

What Changed for Sarah & Emma:

  • Sleep: Emma consistently sleeps 11-12 hours straight every night
  • Bedtime: Takes 5-10 minutes, no crying
  • Naps: Emma now naps independently too (they tackled naps in week 2)
  • Nursing: Sarah still nurses, but it's no longer associated with sleep. She enjoys it again.
  • Relationship: Sarah and her husband have their evenings back. They can talk, watch TV, be a couple again.
  • Energy: Sarah feels like herself again. She's patient, present, and happy.
  • Emma: Happier, less fussy, better mood during the day because she's well-rested

What Sarah Learned

1. Night 3 is Often the Hardest

"Everyone talks about night 1 being the worst," Sarah said. "But for us, night 3 was harder because Emma woke up MORE. I thought I was doing something wrong. But that's the extinction burst - it's actually a sign you're close to a breakthrough."

2. Consistency is Everything

"I wanted to give in so many times," Sarah admitted. "But my husband and I made a pact: we'd stick with it for 5 nights no matter what. If it wasn't working by night 5, we'd try something else. But we had to give it a fair shot."

3. The Feed-to-Sleep Association is Real

"I didn't realize how strong the association was," Sarah explained. "Emma literally didn't know how to fall asleep without nursing. She wasn't hungry at night - she just needed the nursing to fall back asleep. Once we broke that association, everything changed."

4. It's Not Cruel - It's Teaching

"I felt so guilty at first," Sarah said. "But then I realized: I'm teaching Emma a valuable life skill. She's learning to self-soothe. She's learning that sleep is safe and she can do it on her own. That's not cruel - that's parenting."

5. Better Sleep = Better Everything

"I didn't realize how much the sleep deprivation was affecting everything," Sarah reflected. "My relationship with my husband. My patience with Emma. My mental health. Once we were all sleeping, everything improved. I'm a better mom because I'm not exhausted and resentful."

Common Questions Sarah Gets Asked

Q: Did Emma's crying damage your bond?

A: "Not at all. If anything, our bond is stronger now. I'm more patient and present during the day because I'm not exhausted. And Emma is happier because she's well-rested. We both enjoy our time together more."

Q: Do you regret using CIO?

A: "Not for one second. I only regret not doing it sooner. I suffered through 5 months of sleep deprivation when I could have fixed it in a week."

Q: What about sleep regressions?

A: "We've had a few rough nights here and there (teething, illness, etc.). But Emma still knows how to self-soothe. We just stay consistent, and she gets back on track within a night or two."

Q: Would you do it again with another baby?

A: "Absolutely. In fact, I'd do it earlier - around 4 months instead of waiting until 5 months. The earlier you do it, the faster it works."

Sarah's Advice for Parents Considering CIO

  1. Make sure you're ready. You need to be mentally prepared to hear crying. If you're not ready, you'll give in, and that makes it harder.
  2. Get your partner on board. You need support. Have someone stop you from going in when you want to give up.
  3. Pick a start date and commit. Don't start on a whim. Plan it, prepare for it, and commit to at least 5 nights.
  4. Trust the process. Night 3 might be worse than night 1. That's normal. Don't quit right before the breakthrough.
  5. Take care of yourself. Wear headphones. Watch TV. Do something to distract yourself during the crying.
  6. Track your progress. Write down how long the crying lasts each night. You'll see it's getting shorter even when it doesn't feel like it.
  7. Remember why you're doing this. Better sleep = healthier baby, happier family, better quality of life for everyone.
